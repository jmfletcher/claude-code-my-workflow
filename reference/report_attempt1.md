# Infant Mortality in Wisconsin: Evidence, Equity, and Policy

**A report on rates by race and place, evidence-based interventions, and the Birth Equity Act**

Jason Fletcher, University of Wisconsin

March 2026

---

## Executive summary

Wisconsin has among the worst Black–White disparities in infant mortality in the United States; Black infant mortality is about three times the White rate, and Milwaukee has the largest concentration of Black infant deaths in the state. Evidence-based interventions (home visiting, safe sleep, supportive care) can achieve meaningful reductions, but in most of Wisconsin the number of annual deaths is too small to detect such effects in short-term data. **Milwaukee is the best-powered county-level setting** for evaluating whether interventions reduce Black infant mortality; we recommend focusing implementation and impact evaluation there. This report summarizes infant mortality rates by geography and race, links Rep. Shelia Stubbs’s **Birth Equity Act** (seven bills, AB 1082–1088) to the evidence, and notes where support is direct versus indirect.

**Policymakers should be careful when proposing interventions or policies that are very difficult to evaluate.** In settings where infant death counts are low, brief evaluations are often driven by noise and idiosyncratic fluctuation rather than by true program effects. That can suggest a policy is not working when it is, or that a policy worked when it did not—undermining both accountability and public trust. Choosing interventions and geographies where effectiveness can be meaningfully assessed (e.g., Milwaukee for Black infant mortality) improves the chance of learning what works.

## Introduction

Wisconsin has among the worst Black–White disparities in infant mortality in the United States. In 2023 (single-year rates), the state’s Black infant mortality rate was **14.3 deaths per 1,000 live births** compared with **4.4 for White infants**—a more than threefold gap that has persisted for decades (Wisconsin Department of Health Services; Cap Times, 2026). State Rep. Shelia Stubbs has called the situation “shameful and unacceptable,” noting that “Wisconsin is the worst state to raise a Black family in our nation.” Black women in Wisconsin are also at elevated risk of pregnancy-related death: recent Wisconsin data (2020–2022) show pregnancy-related mortality at about **three times** the rate for Non-Hispanic Black birthing people compared with Non-Hispanic White (Wisconsin DHS maternal mortality issue brief).

This report has four parts. **Section 1** summarizes the evidence on interventions that reduce infant mortality and, where available, effect sizes in terms of lives saved or lives saved per dollar. **Section 2** presents infant mortality rates with 95% confidence intervals by geography (State, Milwaukee, Dane, Rest of Wisconsin) and race, using Wisconsin WISH data (summary rates in Table 1 are 2019–2023 averages); it explains why **Milwaukee is the best-powered county-level setting** for evaluating annual Black all-cause infant mortality and argues for **focusing interventions and evaluation on Milwaukee**. **Section 3** summarizes the **Birth Equity Act**—a package of seven bills proposed by Rep. Stubbs (AB 1082–1088)—and links each bill to the evidence in this report. **Section 4** concludes.

## 1. Literature Review: Interventions That Reduce Infant Mortality

A large body of evidence supports the effectiveness of several interventions in reducing infant or neonatal mortality. **Direct evidence from high-income settings** (United States and Europe) most relevant to Wisconsin includes: Nurse-Family Partnership (U.S.), Back to Sleep / Safe to Sleep (U.S.), and the Denmark information campaign (Altindag et al.). **Indirect or mechanism evidence** from low- and middle-income settings (e.g. immediate KMC in Ghana/India/Malawi/Nigeria/Tanzania, CHAMPION in India, Safe Childbirth Checklist in Rajasthan) illustrates effect sizes and types of care but is not a direct transfer to Wisconsin. Global rankings (DCP3 LiST, WHO) emphasize the same *types* of care—care of premature and small newborns, labor and delivery management, neonatal infection treatment, resuscitation, breastfeeding, and safe sleep—but headline counts apply largely to high-burden LMICs. Historically, vaccination has been the single largest infant-life-saving platform globally (Lancet/WHO, 2024); in the U.S., vaccination is already at high coverage, so the remaining gains for a state like Wisconsin lie in **preterm prevention, perinatal and neonatal care quality, safe sleep, and supportive home visiting**. Effect sizes below are reported where possible as numbers of deaths saved or lives saved per dollar. **Outcome alignment:** KMC and some community trials target *neonatal* mortality; safe-sleep campaigns target *SIDS and sleep-related* deaths; NFP affects preventable child deaths and preterm birth. The 17% and 32% benchmarks in Section 2 are used as illustrative *all-cause* infant mortality reductions for power/detectability; cause-specific evaluation (neonatal vs postneonatal, SIDS, injuries) would better match interventions to the outcomes they plausibly move.

**Home visiting by nurses.** The Nurse-Family Partnership (NFP) provides intensive prenatal and postnatal home visits by registered nurses to low-income first-time mothers (typically 25–30 visits over about 17 months). Multiple randomized controlled trials (Elmira, Memphis, Denver) and replications support its effects. In the Memphis trial, there were zero preventable child deaths (SIDS, unintentional injury, homicide) in the nurse-visited group versus 1.6% in the control group over follow-up. A national projection for the 177,517 women enrolled in NFP through 2013 estimated that the program would prevent approximately 500 infant deaths by 2031, along with roughly 10,000 preterm births and 42,000 child maltreatment incidents. Program cost was about $1.6 billion (2010 dollars), with estimated savings from reduced Medicaid, TANF, and food stamps of about $3.0 billion. Cost-effectiveness has been estimated at roughly 0.31 lives saved per $1 million (about $3.2 million per infant death averted) (Miller, 2015; Olds et al.).

**Safe sleep and SIDS campaigns.** In the United States, the Back to Sleep (Safe to Sleep) campaign, beginning in 1994, recommended supine sleep and other safe sleep practices. U.S. SIDS rates fell by more than 50% after the campaign; back sleeping tripled among African American infants. Thousands of infant deaths have been averted over decades, although campaign cost is not attributed per death. In Denmark, a government-sponsored information campaign delivered in part through universal nurse home visiting reduced infant mortality by 17.2%, saving an estimated 11.6–13.5 deaths per 10,000 births. Effects were 11–14 times larger for low-birthweight and preterm infants and for lower-SES families (NICHD Safe to Sleep; Altindag et al., 2022).

**Kangaroo mother care (KMC).** Immediate, continuous skin-to-skin KMC from birth (compared with conventional care first) for low-birthweight infants was evaluated in a multi-country trial (Ghana, India, Malawi, Nigeria, Tanzania) of 3,211 infants. The intervention group had 58 fewer neonatal deaths (12.0% vs 15.7% mortality; relative risk 0.75, 95% CI 0.64–0.89). WHO has estimated that if immediate KMC were adopted globally where only post-stabilization KMC is currently used, approximately 150,000 additional lives could be saved per year. Meta-analyses associate KMC with about 32% lower neonatal mortality (RR ~0.68), with high-certainty evidence (NEJM, 2021; WHO; BMJ Global Health).

**Community-based and facility-based care.** Community health worker–delivered home-based neonatal care has been associated with roughly 25% neonatal mortality reduction (RR ~0.75) in systematic reviews. The CHAMPION cluster randomized trial in India found 92 fewer neonatal deaths (95% CI 31–152) in the intervention arm (24% lower neonatal mortality). In Guinea-Bissau, early BCG and oral polio vaccine at birth resulted in 21 fewer early infant deaths (7 vs 28) in the intervention arm—59% lower early infant mortality. The WHO-style Safe Childbirth Checklist in Rajasthan was associated with about 11% reduction in facility stillbirths and very early neonatal deaths, with scaling estimates on the order of 40,000 intrapartum-related deaths averted per year if implemented across India (PLOS Medicine; BMJ Global Health; MCNP Journal/BMC).

For rate-based comparisons, a 17% reduction on a rate of 5 per 1,000 yields 0.85 per 1,000; on 13 per 1,000 (Black) it yields 2.2 per 1,000. A 32% reduction on 5 per 1,000 yields 1.6 per 1,000; on 13 per 1,000 it yields 4.2 per 1,000. These magnitudes are used in Section 2 to compare with the precision of Wisconsin rate estimates.

## 2. Infant Mortality by Race and Place in Wisconsin

**Data sources and methods.** Data are from WISH (Wisconsin Interactive Statistics on Health): births and infant deaths by mother’s race (Non-Hispanic White, Non-Hispanic Black). Source inputs are WISH query outputs and PDF tables: State and Milwaukee birth counts and infant death counts by race and year; Dane County births and infant death counts by race and year; and the WISH Infant Mortality Module query results. WISH data used here span 1999 through 2023. **Table 1 and Table 2** use rates averaged over the five-year period 2019–2023 with approximate 95% CIs (Wald-style: rate ± 1.96 × (1000√D)/B, where D = deaths, B = births, under a Poisson assumption for counts). **Table 3 and the detectability discussion** use approximate *annual* death counts for one-year policy evaluation (same Poisson logic: CI for count ≈ [D − 1.96√D, D + 1.96√D]). **Rest of Wisconsin** = State − Milwaukee − Dane. When the Black death count is suppressed (e.g. “X” for small Black death counts, typically 1–4), the Black count is imputed as **Total − White** for that table (Total = table total, all races in the WISH query). This report uses only Non-Hispanic White and Non-Hispanic Black. Rates and figures use these imputed counts where applicable.

### 2.1 Wisconsin and national context

Wisconsin has had one of the worst Black infant mortality rates among U.S. states; **Tomlin et al. (2021)** found Wisconsin had the highest Black infant mortality in the nation for their 2011–2016 study period. Using WISH data, they showed that the disparity is driven by two factors: (1) higher rates of premature birth among Black infants and (2) higher mortality among *term* Black infants, with deaths from **SIDS, accidents, and assaults** markedly elevated—pointing to modifiable causes (safe sleep, injury prevention) alongside preterm prevention. Nationally, Black infant mortality remains about twice the White rate (e.g., 10.9 vs 4.5 per 1,000 in 2023), and disparities persist across income and education, reflecting structural racism and access to care rather than biology (KFF, 2025).

Given the number of Black infant deaths in Milwaukee (~55 per year), **Milwaukee is the best-powered county-level geography** for detecting statistically meaningful one-year effects of the size seen in the literature (e.g. a 32% reduction is detectable there; see Table 2 and Table 3). Evaluation in other geographies or statewide may still be feasible with longer follow-up, pooled years, or cause-specific outcomes.

**Findings:** Black infant mortality is about **three times** White in every geography. **Milwaukee has the largest absolute number of Black infant deaths** (~55 per year) and is the only county-level concentration in Wisconsin where Black births and deaths are large enough to support meaningful program evaluation. Dane and Rest of Wisconsin have small Black infant death counts (~6 and ~19 per year), so their Black rates have very wide confidence intervals.

### Table 2. Detectability of rate reductions: margin to lower bound of 95% CI (rate-based)

For a rate reduction to be detectable in one year, the *reduction* (in per 1,000) must be at least as large as the **margin to the lower bound** of the 95% CI (point estimate − lower bound). Otherwise, the post-intervention rate could still fall inside the pre-intervention interval and be attributed to chance. Table 2 uses the same logic as Table 3 (count-based): effect detectable only if it moves the estimate below the lower bound.

| Geography | Race | Avg rate (2019–23) | 95% CI (per 1,000) | Margin to lower bound | 17% reduction | 32% reduction | 17% detectable? | 32% detectable? |
|-----------|------|--------------------|---------------------|------------------------|---------------|---------------|-----------------|------------------|
| State | White | 4.45 | 3.81–5.09 | 0.64 | 0.76 | 1.42 | Yes | Yes |
| State | Black | 13.07 | 10.2–15.9 | 2.87 | 2.22 | 4.18 | No | Yes |
| Milwaukee | White | 4.06 | 2.2–5.9 | 1.86 | 0.69 | 1.30 | No | No |
| **Milwaukee** | **Black** | **13.13** | **9.6–16.7** | **3.53** | **2.23** | **4.20** | **No** | **Yes** |
| Dane | White | 3.49 | 1.9–5.1 | 1.59 | 0.59 | 1.12 | No | No |
| Dane | Black | 11.39 | 2.3–20.5 | 9.09 | 1.94 | 3.64 | No | No |
| Rest of Wisconsin | White | 4.60 | 3.9–5.3 | 0.70 | 0.78 | 1.47 | Yes | Yes |
| Rest of Wisconsin | Black | 13.54 | 7.2–19.9 | 6.34 | 2.30 | 4.33 | No | No |

**Interpretation:** For **Milwaukee Black** (rate 13.13 per 1,000; CI 9.6–16.7), the margin to the lower bound is 3.53 per 1,000. A **32% reduction** (4.20 per 1,000) would be detectable in one year; a **17% reduction** (2.23) would not. This aligns with Table 3: a 32% reduction in 55 deaths is ~18 deaths averted, which exceeds the count-based minimum of 15. Table 2 and Table 3 are therefore consistent. In most other cells, especially Dane and Rest of Wisconsin Black, even a 32% reduction does not clear the margin, so **Milwaukee remains the only county-level geography where a substantial one-year effect on Black infant mortality could be detected**. State-wide aggregation would have more power; evaluation outside Milwaukee may still be feasible with longer follow-up, pooled years, or cause-specific outcomes (e.g. neonatal vs postneonatal). **Recommendation:** Cause-specific Wisconsin data (neonatal vs postneonatal, SIDS, injuries) would allow tighter matching of interventions to the outcomes they affect and would strengthen the report’s policy link; such an analysis is recommended for future work.

**Count-based perspective: when could a reduction in the number of deaths be detected?** Table 2 focuses on rates; in practice, interventions reduce the *number* of infant deaths. For a reduction to be detectable in the data, the number of deaths averted in a year must be large enough that the post-intervention count would fall **below the lower bound** of the 95% confidence interval for the (pre-intervention) annual death count. Otherwise, the observed drop could be attributed to random variation. Under a Poisson model, the 95% CI for the count is approximately [D − 1.96√D, D + 1.96√D], so the **minimum deaths averted to be detectable** in one year is the smallest integer greater than 1.96√D (i.e., the gap between the mean and the lower bound). Table 3 gives this minimum for each geography–race cell, the corresponding 95% CI for the death count, and the estimated spending required to avert that many lives at the Nurse-Family Partnership (NFP) benchmark of about **$3.2 million per infant death averted** (0.31 lives saved per $1 million; Miller, 2015).

### Table 3. Annual infant deaths, minimum detectable effect, and estimated spend (count-based)

| Geography | Race | Approx. annual deaths | 95% CI (deaths) | Min. deaths averted to be detectable (per yr) | Est. spend at NFP rate ($M/yr) |
|-----------|------|------------------------|------------------|-----------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| State | White | 192 | 165–219 | 28 | 90 |
| State | Black | 79 | 62–96 | 18 | 58 |
| Milwaukee | White | 17 | 9–25 | 9 | 29 |
| Milwaukee | Black | 55 | 41–70 | 15 | 48 |
| Dane | White | 14 | 7–21 | 8 | 26 |
| Dane | Black | 6 | 1–11 | 5 | 16 |
| Rest of Wisconsin | White | 162 | 137–187 | 25 | 80 |
| Rest of Wisconsin | Black | 19 | 10–28 | 9 | 29 |

*Note:* "Est. spend at NFP rate" is the spending (in $M per year) to avert the number of lives in the "Min. deaths averted" column at $3.2M per life (NFP benchmark; Miller, 2015). Actual programs may differ in effectiveness and cost.

**Interpretation:** For **Milwaukee Black** infants (55 deaths per year; 95% CI 41–70), at least **15 deaths** must be averted in a year for the observed count to fall below 41 and be distinguishable from chance. At the NFP cost-effectiveness of about $3.2M per life, that implies spending on the order of **$48M per year** (15 × $3.2M) for an effect large enough to be detectable in Milwaukee's Black infant population. Fewer deaths averted (e.g. 9) would bring the count down to 46—still inside the interval 41–70—and could not be confidently attributed to the intervention. In **Dane** (6 Black infant deaths; CI 1–11), the minimum detectable effect is 5 deaths averted, requiring ~$16M/yr at NFP rates, in a population where only 6 deaths occur annually; in **Rest of Wisconsin** Black (19 deaths; CI 10–28), 9 deaths averted (~$29M/yr) would be needed for detectability. **Milwaukee is the only county-level geography** where the concentration of Black infant deaths (55/yr) makes a detectable reduction in *number* of deaths feasible with a single focused program, and even there the required scale of investment ($48M/yr at NFP rates) illustrates the resource commitment needed to both achieve and demonstrate an effect.

## 3. Policy Proposal: The Birth Equity Act

State Rep. Shelia Stubbs (Madison) has introduced the **Birth Equity Act** in multiple sessions to address Wisconsin’s exceedingly high Black infant and maternal mortality rates. As reported by the *Cap Times* (March 12, 2026), the package aims to make “pregnancy, birth, postpartum services and infant care more affordable and accessible” for residents who have faced discrimination and barriers to care; the proposed changes **apply to all residents regardless of race or ethnicity** (Cap Times, 2026).

The current iteration consists of **seven bills** (Assembly Bills 1082–1088), each with cosponsorship from more than a dozen Democrats; as of the report, no Republicans had signed on and the Republican-controlled Assembly had largely adjourned, so the bills were not expected to advance in 2026.

### Summary of the seven bills

| Bill | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **AB 1082** | Sales tax exception for breastfeeding equipment (e.g., breast pump, milk collection and safe storage supplies). |
| **AB 1083** | Bar the state from seeking recovery or reimbursement of birth expenses from a new mom covered by Medicaid. |
| **AB 1084** | Require Medicaid to cover dental services by a dentist or dental therapist; ensure pregnant patients can receive timely dental care during pregnancy. |
| **AB 1085** | Require Medicaid plans to cover care provided by a **doula** before, during, and after delivery. |
| **AB 1086** | Require Medicaid to cover maternal mental health screenings; require the state health department to develop—in consultation with Black community advocates and providers—standards for **cultural competency** in maternal mental health screening. |
| **AB 1087** | Allow a pregnant person to enroll in a health insurance plan **outside of standard enrollment periods** after becoming pregnant so prenatal care is covered. |
| **AB 1088** | Require hospitals to provide a **postpartum home visit** to a new mom and baby, if requested, within the first few weeks after birth; require health plans to cover these visits. Visits may be conducted by a doula, midwife, nurse-midwife, or other licensed provider certified in postpartum care. |

Stubbs has stated that she worked with “doulas, health care providers, birth workers and communities of color” to develop the package and that “those that are impacted are the best voices, and we have to do a better job of listening.”

### Link to evidence in this report

The following summarizes how each bill relates to evidence cited in Section 1 (and elsewhere in this report). Where direct evidence on infant mortality is lacking, that is stated explicitly.

- **AB 1082 (breastfeeding equipment tax exception).** **Supported indirectly:** Breastfeeding is associated with lower infant mortality and morbidity in observational and policy literature; DCP3/LiST ranks breastfeeding promotion among interventions that reduce neonatal and child deaths. This report does not cite a high-income study that quantifies infant deaths averted by equipment subsidies specifically; the link is through support for breastfeeding as a modifiable factor (e.g., WHO; DCP3).

- **AB 1083 (no state recovery of birth expenses from Medicaid enrollees).** **Supported indirectly:** Reducing out-of-pocket and administrative burden for postpartum families is consistent with evidence that coverage and financial barriers affect maternal and infant care use (KFF, 2025). This report does not cite a study that estimates the effect of this specific provision on infant mortality.

- **AB 1084 (Medicaid coverage for dental care in pregnancy).** **Limited direct evidence in this report:** Dental care in pregnancy is a modifiable risk factor for adverse birth outcomes in some studies; the literature review in Section 1 does not include a high-income trial or quasi-experiment that quantifies infant deaths averted by Medicaid dental coverage. The bill addresses a barrier that disproportionately affects low-income and Black families (KFF, 2025).

- **AB 1085 (Medicaid coverage for doula care).** **Supported by analogy; direct mortality evidence limited:** Intensive home visiting by nurses (Nurse-Family Partnership) is supported by RCTs and reduces preventable child deaths and preterm birth (Miller, 2015; Olds et al.). Doula care is a form of supportive, non-clinical care before, during, and after birth. U.S. doula RCTs have shown improvements in process outcomes (e.g., breastfeeding, childbirth preparation) but have not demonstrated a statistically significant effect on infant mortality in the trials summarized in Section 1 and the broader literature. The rationale for AB 1085 is alignment with the *type* of intervention (supportive, person-centered care) that the NFP and similar programs represent, not a direct citation of doula-specific mortality reduction.

- **AB 1086 (Medicaid coverage for maternal mental health screening; cultural competency standards).** **Supported indirectly:** Perinatal depression and mental health disparities are recognized determinants of maternal and infant wellbeing (KFF, 2025). Screening and referral can improve access to treatment. This report does not cite a study that quantifies infant mortality reduction from universal screening or from cultural-competency standards specifically; the support is for addressing a known risk factor and equity gap.

- **AB 1087 (special enrollment for pregnancy in health plans).** **Supported indirectly:** Timely prenatal care is associated with better birth outcomes; barriers to enrollment delay care. KFF and related sources document coverage and access as drivers of maternal and infant health disparities. This report does not cite a study that isolates the effect of pregnancy-related special enrollment on infant mortality.

- **AB 1088 (postpartum home visit, if requested; coverage by plans).** **Most closely aligned with the home-visiting evidence base, though not directly tested in this exact form:** NFP and the Denmark campaign provide intensive or universal nurse home visiting over many contacts; AB 1088 is an optional, short-series postpartum visit. The report does not cite a trial of this specific design. Postpartum home visiting is consistent with programs that have been shown in RCTs to reduce preventable child deaths (Miller, 2015; Olds et al.; Altindag et al., 2022). AB 1088 aligns with that evidence more closely than any other bill in the package, but the strength of support is alignment rather than direct evidence.

Given the data in **Section 2**, concentrating **implementation and evaluation** of such policies in **Milwaukee**—where Black infant deaths are most concentrated—would maximize the chance of detecting whether the package (or components) reduce infant mortality and narrow the Black–White gap.

## 4. Conclusion

- **Wisconsin** has among the worst Black–White infant mortality disparities in the U.S., with Black rates about **three times** White rates in every geography examined (State, Milwaukee, Dane, Rest of Wisconsin). Milwaukee has the **largest number of Black infant deaths** (~55 per year) and thus the best setting for measurable impact and evaluation.
- **Evidence-based interventions**—home visiting (e.g., NFP), safe sleep campaigns, kangaroo mother care, community-based neonatal care—can achieve substantial reductions (e.g., 17–32% in rate terms) and, for NFP, ~0.31 lives saved per $1M. **Milwaukee is the best-powered county-level setting** for evaluating annual Black all-cause infant mortality with one-year data; in most other geographies, even a 32% rate reduction may not be detectable in a single year. Statewide and non-Milwaukee evaluation can still be feasible with longer follow-up, pooled years, cause-specific outcomes (e.g. neonatal vs postneonatal), or stronger designs. **Focusing programs and evaluation on Milwaukee** is the most practical way to generate interpretable evidence on what works with current data.
- The **Birth Equity Act** (Rep. Shelia Stubbs) proposes seven bills to improve affordability and access to prenatal, birth, postpartum, and infant care—including doula coverage, postpartum home visits, dental and mental health care, and Medicaid protections. These align with the types of interventions that the literature associates with improved maternal and infant outcomes. Prioritizing **Milwaukee** for implementation and impact evaluation would best use limited resources and data to close the Black–White gap in Wisconsin.
