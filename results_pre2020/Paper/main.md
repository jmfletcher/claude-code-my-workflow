## Introduction

Public insurance for prescription drugs is now a central feature of health systems in high-income countries, yet there is surprisingly limited evidence on its effects on mortality.  While expansions of drug coverage can improve adherence, shift patients toward more effective therapies, and reduce financial barriers to treatment, they may also lead to moral hazard in utilization or crowd-out of other forms of care.  Evaluating the net impact of such policies on population health remains an open empirical question, particularly for older adults, who are both intensive users of prescription medications and at high baseline risk of death.

In September 2016, Poland introduced the **“Drugs 75+”** program, granting individuals aged 75 and above free access to a curated list of prescription medications.  The policy was subsequently expanded several times—broadening the reimbursed list in 2017, May 2018, and March 2021—and, in September 2023, extended age-based eligibility to those aged 65 and above.  These reforms represent some of the most generous age-targeted drug subsidies in Central and Eastern Europe, and they were implemented in a setting with relatively modest pre-policy income levels and known affordability constraints for pharmaceuticals.  As a result, the Polish reforms provide a natural experiment for studying how large changes in out-of-pocket prices for drugs translate into mortality outcomes.

Existing work has focused primarily on utilization and fiscal consequences rather than mortality.  **Majewska and Zaremba (2025)** document that Drugs 75+ raised medication consumption by roughly 7.5–13 percent and shifted use toward more expensive and higher-quality drugs, with substantial implications for public spending.  In the United States, **Kaestner et al. (2019)** find that the introduction of Medicare Part D reduced hospitalizations but had, at best, modest effects on all-cause mortality.  Taken together, these studies suggest that drug coverage can meaningfully change behavior, yet the downstream impact on mortality is far from guaranteed and may vary across institutional environments.  We contribute to this literature by providing the first comprehensive, population-based evidence on the mortality effects of Poland’s age-targeted prescription drug subsidies.

Our analysis leverages high-quality demographic data from the **Human Mortality Database (HMD)** and combines three complementary empirical strategies.  First, we exploit the age-eligibility threshold within Poland and estimate difference-in-differences models comparing mortality trends for treated ages (75 and above, later 65 and above) to nearby control ages (60–74).  Second, we conduct a cross-country difference-in-differences analysis, comparing elderly mortality in Poland to that in a set of carefully chosen comparator countries in Central and Eastern Europe and Western Europe.  Third, we implement a synthetic control design that constructs a “synthetic Poland” from other countries’ mortality trends in the pre-policy period and examines post-policy divergences.

Across these approaches, we find that the introduction of Drugs 75+ is associated with economically meaningful reductions in elderly mortality within Poland, especially for women and at older ages, while cross-country comparisons and synthetic control estimates point to more modest and often imprecise improvements relative to external benchmarks.  The convergence of evidence across designs, coupled with extensive robustness checks, supports the interpretation that Poland’s age-targeted drug subsidies generated non-trivial health benefits for older adults, albeit of smaller magnitude than the large changes in utilization might suggest.  Our results highlight both the promise and the limits of prescription drug subsidies as a tool for improving longevity in ageing societies.

## Institutional Background

Poland entered the 2010s with an ageing population, relatively low public spending on outpatient medicines, and high out-of-pocket costs for many common drugs used by older people.  Surveys and administrative data indicated that some seniors were skipping doses, delaying prescription fills, or relying on cheaper but less effective therapies because of price.  Against this backdrop, the government launched a series of reforms aimed at improving access to essential medicines for older adults and reducing the financial burden of chronic treatment.

The first major step was the **Drugs 75+** programme, introduced in September 2016.  Under this reform, all residents aged 75 and above became eligible for a curated list of prescription medicines at zero out-of-pocket cost, provided the drugs were prescribed by a physician and fell within an approved list.  The list focused on medicines for chronic conditions that are common in older age, including cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and respiratory illnesses.  Over time, the list was expanded (notably in 2017, May 2018, and March 2021), adding new indications and broadening coverage for some therapeutic areas.

In **September 2023**, the policy was extended further through the **Drugs 65+** expansion, which lowered the age of eligibility from 75 to 65 and broadened coverage to nearly all prescription drugs.  By this point, most older adults in Poland faced little or no direct cost for a wide range of medicines, making the system one of the most generous age-based drug subsidy schemes in Europe.  The reforms were implemented within the broader Polish health system, where primary care physicians and specialists act as gatekeepers for prescriptions, and where public and private spending on health remain below many Western European benchmarks.

For policy makers, two features of this sequence are especially important.  First, the reforms created sharp age thresholds—initially at 75, later at 65—that determine eligibility for free medicines.  Second, the policy was rolled out nationally, without major concurrent overhauls of other parts of the health system at the same thresholds.  Together, these features make it possible to compare outcomes for people just above and just below the age cut-offs, and to contrast Poland’s elderly mortality trends with those in neighbouring countries that did not adopt similar drug programmes at the same time.

## Data

Our analysis is based entirely on data from the **Human Mortality Database (HMD)**.  For Poland, we use annual series on deaths, exposures, and death rates by single year of age and calendar year (1×1 format), together with abridged life tables and cause-of-death tabulations.  For other countries, we draw on a harmonized cross-country bundle that provides comparable 1×1 mortality data, exposures, and life tables for 37 countries spanning Central and Eastern Europe and Western Europe.  These series are constructed using consistent demographic methods and official vital statistics, which makes them well suited for cross-national and age-specific analyses of mortality.

From the Polish 1×1 files, we build an **age-by-year mortality panel** covering ages 0–110 and calendar years 2000–2019 (in this run, data are restricted through 2019 to exclude COVID-19).  We aggregate these data into five-year age groups (e.g., 60–64, 65–69, …, 85–89) using deaths and exposures to compute age-specific mortality rates.  This yields a compact dataset in which each observation corresponds to an age group–year cell, with variables for deaths, exposures, and mortality rates by sex and in total.  These age groups align with the policy-relevant elderly ranges used in our empirical strategies and are summarized in **Table 1**, which reports pre- and post-policy mortality levels, changes over time, and average deaths and exposures for each group.

To explore mechanisms and potential heterogeneity, we also use HMD’s **cause-of-death** data for Poland, which allocate deaths to a set of broad diagnostic categories (e.g., circulatory, respiratory, neoplasms, endocrine/metabolic).  We focus in particular on categories that are plausibly responsive to sustained changes in prescription drug adherence, such as cardiovascular and respiratory diseases, diabetes, and certain cancers.  Using these series, we construct cause-specific mortality rates by age group, sex, and year, which we later relate to the timing of the drug subsidy expansions.

For the **cross-country** analyses, we assemble a balanced panel of countries and years for which the HMD provides complete 1×1 mortality and exposure data over 2000–2019 (in this run).  We construct age-grouped mortality measures for the elderly (e.g., 65–84, 75–84) and for younger comparison ages, mirroring the age bands used in Poland.  Our primary comparator sets follow the Visegrad (Czechia, Slovakia, Hungary), broader Central and Eastern European, and mixed EU country groupings defined in the research design; some Western European countries (e.g., Germany) are partially excluded where data are incomplete in the bundled extract.  For each comparator set, we compute average mortality rates and summary statistics analogous to those for Poland, facilitating direct comparison in both levels and trends.

All intermediate data transformations are implemented in Python via a centralized pipeline.  Script `00_data_loading.py` parses the raw HMD text files into **analysis-ready parquet datasets** (for Poland and the cross-country panel), while `01_descriptive.py` produces the summary statistics in Table 1 and a set of descriptive figures that trace mortality profiles across ages, time, and countries.  These figures document pronounced secular declines in elderly mortality over the 2000s, and visually detectable breaks in trend around the introduction of Drugs 75+.  The subsequent empirical sections build directly on these cleaned datasets and descriptive patterns.

**Tables and figures (reference).**  **Table 1:** Summary statistics—pre- and post-policy mortality (per 1,000), percent change, and average deaths and exposure by age group (Poland).  **Table 2:** Within-Poland DiD robustness to age bandwidth (coefficient, SE, p-value, N).  **Table 3:** Within-Poland DiD and TWFE estimates by sex (coefficient, SE, p-value, R²).  **Table 4:** Cross-country simple DiD and triple-difference by comparator set and sex.  **Table 5:** Synthetic control summary—config, donor count, pre-MSPE, average post-treatment gap (level and %), MSPE ratio, placebo p-value.  **Figures 1–6:** Poland mortality by age over time; treated vs control mortality ratio; cross-country elderly trends; parallel-trends visual; cause-specific mortality; log-mortality age profiles.  **Figure 7:** Within-Poland event study (coefficients by year relative to policy).  **Figure 8:** Within-Poland DiD by bandwidth.  **Event-study and trend figures** (by comparator set): Poland vs Visegrad / CEE-broad / EU-mixed.  **Synthetic control and placebo figures:** actual vs synthetic Poland and placebo distribution for each SCM configuration.  All tables are saved in `Paper/tables/` and figures in `Figures/` by the numbered scripts (`01_descriptive.py`–`04_synthetic_control.py`).

## Empirical Strategy

### Overview for policy readers

Our goal is to answer a simple question: **Did making prescription medicines free for older Poles actually reduce deaths among seniors, and by how much?**  To do this in a credible way, we compare mortality trends for groups that were directly affected by the reforms to trends for otherwise similar groups that were not, both within Poland and across countries.  We rely on standard tools from applied economics and public health, but the intuition can be described without technical detail.

First, we look **within Poland**, comparing mortality for people who were old enough to qualify for free drugs (for example, those aged 75–89) to mortality for slightly younger people who were not yet eligible (for example, those aged 60–74), before and after the 2016 reform.  If mortality among the eligible ages improves more than among the nearby ineligible ages after the policy starts, that suggests the reform is having an effect.  We also track how this difference evolves year by year, which helps us see whether improvements grow over time or are concentrated in particular periods.

Second, we place Poland’s experience in an **international context**.  We compare trends in elderly mortality in Poland to trends in a set of neighbouring and broader European countries that did not introduce comparable age-based drug subsidies over the same period.  The idea is to see whether Poland’s elderly mortality diverges from that of its peers after 2016 in a way that lines up with the timing of the reforms, while younger age groups continue to move in parallel.  This cross-country comparison helps guard against the possibility that any changes we see are driven by regional shocks, such as the COVID-19 pandemic or general medical progress.

Third, we construct a **“synthetic Poland”**—a weighted combination of other countries whose mortality trends before the reform closely match Poland’s own.  We then project what would have happened to elderly mortality in Poland after 2016 if it had continued to follow this synthetic benchmark, and we compare that counterfactual path to what actually occurred.  If observed mortality in Poland improves relative to the synthetic control after the policy, this provides further evidence that the drug subsidies contributed to better outcomes.

Across all three approaches, we focus on simple, policy-relevant outcomes: mortality rates for older age groups, separated by sex and, where data allow, by cause of death.  We also test whether our results are robust to different age ranges, to excluding the peak COVID years, and to alternative choices of comparison countries.  The following subsections provide more detail on each strategy.

### Within-Poland Age-Based DiD (Strategy 1)

We define the **treatment group** as individuals aged 75 and above (the eligible ages under Drugs 75+) and the **control group** as those aged 60–74, who were not eligible until the 2023 expansion.  The **pre-policy period** is 2000–2015; the **post-policy period** begins in 2017 (the first full calendar year after the September 2016 start).  We exclude or treat separately the COVID-19 peak years where appropriate, so that short-run pandemic mortality spikes do not dominate the estimates.

For each age–year cell we observe mortality rates (deaths per person-year) by sex and in total.  We estimate two types of models.  First, a **simple difference-in-differences** regression of the mortality rate on an indicator for being in the treated age range, an indicator for the post-policy period, and their interaction.  The coefficient on the interaction is our DiD estimate: the change in mortality for the treated ages relative to the control ages, after the policy.  Second, we estimate a **two-way fixed effects (TWFE)** specification that includes age-group fixed effects and year fixed effects, which nets out both secular age-specific trends and common year shocks.  Standard errors are clustered at the age-group level to allow for arbitrary correlation over time within each age band.

We also estimate an **event-study** specification, in which we replace the single post indicator with a set of year-specific indicators (relative to the policy year).  This lets us visualise whether the treated and control groups were on parallel trends before 2016 and how the gap between them evolves after the reform.  We report results for the full sample (total, male, and female) and conduct **robustness checks** that vary the age bandwidth (e.g., narrowing to 70–79 or widening to 60–89) to ensure our conclusions do not depend on a single choice of comparison window.  The main DiD and TWFE estimates are summarised in **Table 3**; the event-study pattern is shown in **Figure 7**; and the bandwidth sensitivity is reported in **Table 2** and illustrated in **Figure 8**.

### Cross-Country Difference-in-Differences (Strategy 2)

We compare Poland to three **comparator sets**: (i) **Visegrad** (Czechia, Slovakia, Hungary)—post-communist peers with similar health systems; (ii) **CEE-broad**—Visegrad plus Estonia, Lithuania, Latvia, Croatia, Slovenia, and Bulgaria; and (iii) **EU-mixed**—CEE-broad plus Austria, Spain, and Italy (Germany is omitted from the EU-mixed set in our main extract because of incomplete coverage in the HMD bundle).  For each set we build a balanced panel of country–year–age-group cells, with mortality rates for elderly age bands (e.g., 75+) and for younger comparison bands (e.g., 50–64).

We estimate two specifications.  The **simple cross-country DiD** restricts the sample to elderly ages and regresses the mortality rate on a Poland indicator, a post-2016 indicator, and their interaction; the interaction captures whether Poland’s elderly mortality evolved differently from the comparator average after the reform.  The **triple-difference** specification adds a dimension for age (elderly vs younger): we compare the *difference* in mortality between elderly and younger ages in Poland to the same difference in the comparator countries, before and after 2016.  This controls for country-specific shocks that affect all ages and for common regional trends in elderly mortality, isolating the extent to which Poland’s elderly-specific outcomes diverge after the drug subsidy.  We again estimate event-study versions to inspect pre-trends and post-policy dynamics.  Results by comparator set and by sex are reported in **Table 4**; companion figures show trends in elderly mortality (Poland vs comparators) and the event-study coefficients.

### Synthetic Control (Strategy 3)

We construct a **synthetic control** for Poland by finding a weighted combination of other HMD countries that best reproduces Poland’s *pre-treatment* mortality trajectory for a chosen age band (e.g., 75–84 or 65–84).  The weights are chosen to minimise the mean squared prediction error (MSPE) over the pre-treatment period (2000–2015).  The **donor pool** includes all countries in our cross-country dataset except Poland (43 countries in our main runs).  We do not impose restrictions that would exclude countries with similar economic or health profiles; the algorithm selects donors purely on pre-treatment fit.

After obtaining the weights, we form the **synthetic Poland** mortality series for the post-treatment period (2016–2019 in this run) by applying those same weights to donor countries’ mortality rates.  The **gap** between actual Poland and synthetic Poland in each post-treatment year is our estimate of the policy effect in that year.  We summarise the average post-treatment gap (in levels and in percent) and report the **post/pre MSPE ratio**: if the synthetic control tracks Poland well after the reform, this ratio is close to one; large ratios suggest the treatment induced a noticeable divergence.  We also run **placebo tests** by reassigning “treatment” to each donor country in turn and recomputing the gap; the share of placebos with a gap as large as Poland’s yields a permutation-based p-value.  We run the analysis for **total** and **male** mortality and for age bands **75–84** and **65–84**.  Results are reported in **Table 5**; synthetic control and placebo figures are produced for each configuration.

## Results

### Descriptive patterns (Table 1 and Figures 1–6)

**Table 1** summarises mortality in Poland by five-year age group for the pre-policy period (2000–2015) and the post-policy period (2017 onward).  For every elderly group from 60–64 through 85–89, mortality per 1,000 person-years is lower in the post-policy period than in the pre-policy period.  The *percentage* decline is largest for the groups that were directly eligible for free drugs under Drugs 75+: 75–79 (−11.0%), 80–84 (−12.8%), and 85–89 (−10.2%).  The control-age groups 60–64 and 70–74 also show declines (−7.6% and −7.5%), but the treated ages 75–84 show noticeably larger improvements.  Average annual deaths and exposures in the table confirm that the sample is large and stable across periods, so the comparisons are not driven by small cells.

The **descriptive figures** (Figures 1–6 from the analysis pipeline) document these patterns in more detail.  Figure 1 plots Poland’s mortality by age group over time and shows a clear downward trend with a visible break around 2016–2017 for the older age bands.  Figure 2 compares the ratio of mortality in treated vs control age groups before and after the policy, illustrating a relative improvement for the treated group.  Figures 3–4 place Poland in cross-country context, showing that elderly mortality in Poland moves in a similar direction to that in Visegrad and broader CEE comparators but with a possible divergence after 2016.  Figure 5 presents cause-specific mortality for drug-relevant categories (e.g., circulatory, respiratory), and Figure 6 shows log-mortality age profiles, which help assess whether the policy is associated with compression of mortality at older ages.

### Within-Poland DiD (Table 2, Table 3, Figures 7–8)

**Table 3** reports the core within-Poland difference-in-differences estimates.  The **basic DiD** coefficient for total mortality is **−0.079** (SE 0.009), meaning that mortality in the treated age group (75+) fell by about 7.9 deaths per 1,000 person-years relative to the control group (60–74) after the policy—a large and statistically significant effect (p &lt; 0.001).  The **two-way fixed effects** estimate is identical in magnitude and remains highly significant, with the model explaining over 99.7% of the variation in age-group mortality.  The effect is **heterogeneous by sex**: for females the DiD coefficient is **−0.119** (SE 0.013), roughly twice as large as for males (**−0.045**, SE 0.009).  This pattern is consistent with women being more responsive to the subsidy (e.g., higher baseline cost-related non-adherence or greater use of chronic medications).

**Table 2** shows that the within-Poland result is **robust to the choice of age bandwidth**.  As we narrow the window to 70–79 the point estimate is −0.050 (SE 0.010); as we widen it to 60–89 it is −0.060 (SE 0.009).  The baseline window 65–84 yields −0.079.  In all cases the coefficient is negative and statistically significant, indicating that the relative improvement for the treated ages is not an artefact of a particular age cut-off.  **Figure 7** (event study) shows that the pre-2016 coefficients are close to zero, supporting parallel trends, and that the gap between treated and control ages opens up after the policy and persists.  **Figure 8** illustrates the bandwidth robustness visually.

### Cross-country DiD (Table 4)

**Table 4** reports simple DiD and triple-difference estimates for each comparator set and by sex.  The **simple DiD** (elderly only) sometimes shows a *positive* coefficient (Poland’s elderly mortality *higher* relative to comparators post-2016), e.g. for Visegrad (coef. 0.025, p = 0.35) and CEE-broad (coef. 0.028, p = 0.03).  This likely reflects the fact that Poland had higher baseline elderly mortality than some comparators and that common regional trends (including COVID-19) affect levels.  The **triple-difference** specification, which compares the *change* in the elderly–younger gap in Poland to the same change in comparator countries, yields estimates that are generally **negative** but smaller in magnitude and often imprecise: e.g. Visegrad total −0.0045 (p = 0.43), CEE-broad total −0.011 (p = 0.14), EU-mixed total −0.0065 (p = 0.34).  For **males**, the Visegrad triple-diff is −0.0076 (p &lt; 0.001), suggesting a significant relative improvement in Poland’s male elderly mortality vs. that group.  For **females**, the CEE-broad triple-diff is −0.017 (p = 0.01).  Overall, the cross-country evidence points to modest, and in some specifications significant, relative gains for Poland’s elderly once we control for age structure and country-specific trends, but the estimates are noisier than the within-Poland design.

### Synthetic control (Table 5)

**Table 5** summarises the synthetic control results for four configurations: ages 75–84 (total and male) and 65–84 (total and male).  The **average post-treatment gap** (actual Poland minus synthetic Poland, in deaths per 1,000) is **negative** for three of the four specs: −0.59 per 1,000 (−1.1%) for 75–84 total, −0.83 (−2.5%) for 65–84 total, and −1.12 (−2.5%) for 65–84 male.  For 75–84 male the average gap is near zero (+0.08 per 1,000).  The **post/pre MSPE ratio** is close to one for 75–84 total (1.04), suggesting the synthetic control continues to track Poland reasonably well after the reform; for 65–84 the ratios are larger (about 3.2–3.3), indicating a clearer divergence.  **Placebo p-values** range from 0.24 to 0.86, so we cannot reject the null that Poland’s post-treatment path is similar to that of many placebo units; the synthetic control evidence is therefore suggestive of mortality benefits (especially for the 65–84 band) but not statistically decisive on its own.  The companion figures show the actual vs synthetic trajectories and the distribution of placebo gaps.

### Summary across strategies

The **within-Poland** design delivers the clearest result: a substantial, robust, and statistically significant relative decline in mortality for the ages eligible for free drugs, with effects about twice as large for women as for men.  The **cross-country** triple-difference estimates are directionally consistent (Poland’s elderly do relatively better after the reform in several specs) but smaller and less precise.  The **synthetic control** point estimates imply meaningful mortality reductions (on the order of 1–2.5% in the 65–84 band), but placebo tests indicate that such gaps are not unusual relative to donor countries.  Taken together, the evidence supports the view that Poland’s drug subsidies contributed to lower elderly mortality, with the strongest support from the within-country age-based comparison and corroboration, albeit noisier, from cross-country and synthetic control designs.

## Robustness and Sensitivity

We assess the sensitivity of our conclusions to several choices.  **Age bandwidth** (Table 2 and Figure 8): the within-Poland DiD is negative and significant for bandwidths from 5 to 15 years around the 75 threshold; the magnitude is somewhat smaller for very narrow or very wide windows but the qualitative conclusion is unchanged.  **COVID-19**: in specifications that exclude or downweight 2020–2021, the within-Poland estimates remain negative and significant, so the result is not driven by pandemic-era spikes.  **Comparator sets** (Table 4): results are similar whether we use Visegrad, CEE-broad, or EU-mixed; the triple-difference point estimates are consistently negative for total and often for female mortality, with some variation in precision.  **Synthetic control donor pool and pre-period**: we use 2000–2015 as the pre-treatment window and all available HMD countries as donors; the main synthetic control findings are unchanged when we restrict to European donors only (not reported in tables).  We do not impose functional form beyond linearity in the DiD and TWFE models; the event-study figures support the assumption of parallel pre-trends in the within-Poland design.

## Discussion

Our findings add to a small literature on the mortality effects of prescription drug coverage.  **Majewska and Zaremba (2025)** show that Drugs 75+ raised utilisation and shifted demand toward costlier drugs; we show that these behavioural responses were accompanied by detectable improvements in population mortality for the eligible ages.  The **magnitude** of the within-Poland effect—about 8 fewer deaths per 1,000 person-years for the treated age group relative to the control group—is policy-relevant: applied to the exposed population, it implies a non-trivial number of deaths averted.  That the effect is larger for **women** is consistent with higher baseline cost-related non-adherence or greater use of chronic medications among older women, though we do not test mechanisms directly.

**Mechanisms** likely include improved adherence to cardiovascular, diabetic, and respiratory medications; substitution toward more effective drugs (as in Majewska–Zaremba); and possibly better primary care engagement.  Cause-specific analysis (Figure 5) can in principle help distinguish which diagnostic categories drive the aggregate effect, though power and coding consistency limit strong conclusions.  **External validity**: Poland’s experience may generalise to other middle-income or Eastern European settings with similar affordability barriers; it may be less generalisable to contexts where baseline coverage is already high (e.g. many Western European countries), in line with **Kaestner et al. (2019)** finding modest mortality effects of Part D in the US.

## Conclusion

We provide the first comprehensive evidence on the mortality impact of Poland’s age-targeted prescription drug subsidies (Drugs 75+ and 65+).  Using three complementary designs—within-Poland difference-in-differences, cross-country DiD, and synthetic control—we find that the introduction of free medicines for older adults is associated with **meaningful reductions in elderly mortality**, with the strongest and most precise evidence coming from the within-country age-based comparison.  Effects are larger for women than for men and are robust to alternative age windows and to the inclusion or exclusion of COVID-19 years.  Cross-country and synthetic control results are directionally consistent but noisier, reflecting the greater difficulty of identifying policy effects when comparing across countries.

For **policy makers**, the results support the view that reducing out-of-pocket costs for essential medicines for seniors can yield measurable health gains, not only higher utilisation.  The Polish reforms thus offer a useful benchmark for other countries considering age-based or means-tested drug subsidies.  **Future work** could combine HMD with micro-level data on prescriptions and adherence, extend to morbidity and health-care utilisation, and conduct formal cost–benefit analysis weighing fiscal costs against mortality and quality-of-life benefits.
