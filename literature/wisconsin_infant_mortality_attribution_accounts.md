# Wisconsin infant mortality: policy and newspaper accounts attributing change to programs

This note summarizes **policy and newspaper accounts** in which a specific program, intervention, or process was suggested or credited with changes in infant mortality (or related birth outcomes) in Wisconsin. It does not assess causal validity—only what was claimed or suggested in the source.

---

## 1. Dane County 1990–2007: “Mystery” decline (no single program credited)

**Source:** CDC MMWR (Schlenker et al.), *Newsweek*, NYT-style coverage.

**What changed:** Black infant mortality in Dane County fell ~67% between 1990–2001 and 2002–2007 (from ~19.4 to ~6.4 per 1,000), achieving parity with White rates—the first documented closing of the Black–White gap in any U.S. county or state.

**What was *not* credited:**  
- **CDC MMWR (2009)** stated that “no significant changes in local health-care systems, infrastructure, or practice that correspond to these improvements have been identified.”  
- **Healthy Start (Medicaid for pregnant women)** was considered but *not* credited as the main cause: it was statewide and other Wisconsin counties with similar populations did not see similar declines.

**What was *suggested* (not proven):**  
- **Newsweek (Conant, ~2009)** reported investigators’ hypotheses:  
  - **Harambee (South Madison Health & Family Center)** — “one-stop” center (clinics, Head Start, library, parenting/breastfeeding/bereavement programs). CDC’s Cynthia Ferre: “I’m suspicious that this type of clinic—Harambee—could have affected the rate.” Dr. Thomas Schlenker (Madison–Dane County Public Health): Harambee “is certainly part of the answer. It got agencies to work in a synergistic way, drew the community together, and made people feel valued.”  
  - **Home-visiting nurses** — “some 60 public-health nurses” in the county, with about a dozen on duty for home visits before and after birth for high-risk pregnancies; no one was “exactly sure yet what did the trick.”  
  - **“Access plus trust”** — UW’s Lorraine Lathen and others cited non-health factors (trust, feeling cared for, absence of racial stereotyping in care) as potentially influencing outcomes.

**Official caveat:** The MMWR noted that the trend was based on small absolute numbers (~3 fewer infant deaths per year) and that conclusions should be considered preliminary; factors such as “organizational support and advocacy for black women and families, social inclusion, and improved economic status” were flagged for further study rather than attributed to a single program.

- **CDC MMWR:** [Apparent Disappearance of the Black-White Infant Mortality Gap — Dane County, Wisconsin, 1990–2007](https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/preview/mmwrhtml/mm5820a2.htm)  
- **Newsweek:** Eve Conant, “A Good Mystery: Infant-Mortality Drop in Wisconsin” (e.g. [newsweek.com/good-mystery-infant-mortality-drop-wisconsin-81139](https://www.newsweek.com/good-mystery-infant-mortality-drop-wisconsin-81139))  
- **NYT:** “Trying to Explain a Drop in Infant Mortality” (Nov 2009) — same episode.

## 2. State-level programs cited by DHS (no specific outcome attribution)

**Source:** Kids Forward summary of DHS press release (Sept 2012); DHS Infant Mortality Awareness Month.

**What was cited:** The State Health Officer (Dr. Henry Anderson) listed programs the state is “involved in” to reduce infant mortality:  
- Collaboration with Dept. of Children and Families on **home visiting**  
- Partnership with **Lifecourse Initiative for Healthy Families (LIHF)** (UW School of Medicine and Public Health), a $10 million multi-year effort to reduce racial/ethnic disparities in birth outcomes  
- Working with HMOs in southeast Wisconsin to promote **medical homes for high-risk pregnant women** (care coordination, home visits)  
- **Strive to Quit** (smoking cessation for Medicaid-eligible individuals)  
- **Breastfeeding** promotion  

**Attribution:** The release highlighted trends and disparities; it did not attribute a specific decline in infant mortality to any one of these programs.

- **Kids Forward:** [DHS Highlights Recommendations for Reducing Infant Mortality](https://kidsforward.org/dhs-highlights-recommendations-for-reducing-infant-mortality/)  
- **DHS:** Press release 092412 (linked from Kids Forward).

## 3. Dane County: Saving Our Babies / ConnectRx Wisconsin (program credited by partners)

**Source:** Dane County Health Council (DCHC), Foundation for Black Women’s Wellness (FFBWW), and partners; press and program announcements.

**Programs:**  
- **Saving Our Babies Initiative** — Launched 2018 by DCHC, FFBWW, EQT By Design, Black Maternal Child Health Alliance. Focus: group prenatal care, doula support, SDOH screenings, community-driven support for Black women and families in Dane County.  
- **ConnectRx Wisconsin** — Launched 2022; care coordination connecting Black pregnant women to health care and resources (food, housing, employment, transportation).

**Outcomes credited to the initiative (by program/partners):**  
- 93% of infants born at healthy birth weight (vs. countywide 85%)  
- 93% of births at optimal gestational age (vs. countywide 86%)  
- 396 births supported; 241 doula-supported births; 12,000+ SDOH screenings; 3,900 referrals for support; 1,068 ConnectRx referrals  

**Attribution:** Partners and health system representatives state that the initiative is “making a measurable and positive impact” and that “community-driven strategies … are working.” These are program-level outcome reports, not independent impact evaluations of infant mortality rates.

- **FFBWW:** [Dane County Health Council and Partners Celebrate 7 Years of Transforming Birth Outcomes for Black Families](https://www.ffbww.org/news/dane-county-health-council-and-partners-celebrate-7-years-of-transforming-birth-outcomes-for-black-families-in-dane-county) (May 2025)  
- **Saving Our Babies:** [savingourbabieswi.org](https://www.savingourbabieswi.org/)  
- **Cap Times:** ConnectRx aims to lower Black infant mortality rates, support Black mothers.

## 4. Milwaukee / statewide: Program fraud and crackdown (negative attribution)

**Source:** Milwaukee Journal Sentinel (2022–2023).

**What was reported:** A Journal Sentinel investigation exposed **fraud in Wisconsin’s prenatal care coordination program** (infant mortality prevention). The state subsequently cracked down on fraudulent operators and overhauled the program. The *article* is credited with prompting the policy response, not with reducing infant mortality; the implication is that the *program as previously implemented* was not functioning as intended.

- **JS:** e.g. “Wisconsin officials crack down on fraud-ridden infant mortality program” (Nov 2023).
