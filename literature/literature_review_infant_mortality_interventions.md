# Literature Review: Successful Interventions to Reduce Infant Mortality and Effect Sizes (Deaths Saved)

This review summarizes recent evidence on interventions that reduce infant or neonatal mortality, with effect sizes expressed where possible as **numbers of infant deaths saved** (or preventable). **The emphasis is on interventions with evidence in high-income settings (United States and Europe)** that are relevant to Wisconsin; global rankings and LMIC studies are summarized for context only.

---

## Global context and focus on high-income settings

**Global rankings (LMICs).** The Disease Control Priorities 3 (DCP3) Lives Saved Tool (LiST) models the **immediate annual neonatal deaths averted** if each intervention were scaled to **90% coverage** across **75 high-burden countries** that account for most global maternal and child deaths. In that framework, the largest gains come from: **care of premature babies**; **labor and delivery management** (also stillbirths and maternal deaths); **treatment for neonatal sepsis/pneumonia**; **neonatal resuscitation**; **clean postnatal practices**; **chlorhexidine cord care**; **balanced energy supplementation in pregnancy**; **breastfeeding promotion**; **folic acid supplementation/fortification**; and **clean birth practices**. An Every Newborn–style analysis similarly points to **care during labour and childbirth** and **care of small and ill newborns** as the largest sources of avertable neonatal deaths if interventions were scaled (DCP3 RMNCH Ch1; Healthy Newborn Network). These rankings apply to **low- and middle-income countries**; the same *types* of care (prematurity, delivery quality, neonatal infection, resuscitation, breastfeeding, safe sleep) are priorities in high-income settings, but **effect sizes and cost-effectiveness for the U.S. and Wisconsin must be drawn from high-income evidence**.

**Vaccination.** Historically, vaccination has been the single largest infant-life-saving platform globally. A 2024 Lancet/WHO analysis estimated that vaccination since 1974 averted **101 million infant deaths** and accounted for about **40% of the global decline in infant mortality**. In high-income countries, vaccination coverage is already high, so the remaining gains for a state like Wisconsin lie in **preterm prevention, perinatal and neonatal care quality, safe sleep, and supportive home visiting** (Lancet, 2024).

**Scope of this review.** Sections 1–7 below emphasize **U.S. and European** interventions (NFP, Back to Sleep, Denmark campaign, KMC where applicable in HIC, U.S. equity initiatives). LMIC studies (e.g., CHAMPION, BCG/OPV, Safe Childbirth Checklist) are included briefly for completeness but are not the basis for Wisconsin-specific effect-size or cost comparisons.

## 1. Home visiting by nurses (United States)

### Nurse-Family Partnership (NFP)

**Design:** Intensive prenatal and postnatal home visits by registered nurses to low-income first-time mothers (typically 25–30 visits over ~17 months).

**Evidence:** Multiple RCTs (Elmira, Memphis, Denver) and replications; a systematic projection of outcomes for all U.S. enrollees.

**Effect sizes (infant deaths saved):**

- **Memphis RCT:** Over follow-up, **zero preventable child deaths** (SIDS, unintentional injury, homicide) in the nurse-visited group vs **1.6%** in the control group—a large relative reduction, though absolute numbers were small in the trial.
- **National projection (1996–2013 enrollments):** Among 177,517 women enrolled in NFP through 2013, the program is projected to prevent approximately **500 infant deaths** by 2031. The same cohort is also projected to prevent ~10,000 preterm births, ~42,000 child maltreatment incidents, and other outcomes. Program cost was estimated at ~$1.6 billion (2010 dollars) with ~$3.0 billion in estimated savings from reduced Medicaid, TANF, and food stamps.

**Sources:** Olds et al. (RCTs); Miller (2015), *Prev Sci*, “Projected Outcomes of Nurse-Family Partnership Home Visitation During 1996–2013, United States” (PMC4512284); JAMA Pediatrics and NFP published research on preventable death.

## 2. Safe sleep / SIDS campaigns

### Back to Sleep / Safe to Sleep (United States)

**Design:** National information campaign (from 1994) recommending supine sleep and other safe sleep practices to reduce SIDS and sleep-related infant deaths.

**Effect:** U.S. SIDS rates **fell by more than 50%** after the campaign; back sleeping increased sharply, including a tripling among African American infants. No single study attributes an exact “deaths saved” number to the campaign; the decline in SIDS from the pre-1994 level to current levels implies **thousands of infant deaths averted** over the past three decades. Other sleep-related causes (e.g., accidental suffocation) have partly offset this in terms of total SUID; ~3,400 SUID deaths still occur annually.

**Sources:** NICHD Safe to Sleep®; evidence-based safe sleep program evaluations (e.g., PMC12062421); AAP policy and advocacy.

### Government-sponsored information campaign (Denmark)

**Design:** Nationwide guidelines (from 1991) recommending back/side sleep, disseminated in part through universal nurse home visiting.

**Effect sizes (infant deaths saved):**

- **Overall:** Infant mortality reduced by **17.2%**; estimated **11.6–13.5 infant deaths averted per 10,000 births**.
- **Vulnerable subgroups:** Effects were **11–14 times larger** for low-birthweight and preterm infants and concentrated among lower socioeconomic groups.

**Source:** Altindag, Greve, Tekin, “Public Health Policy at Scale: Impact of a Government-Sponsored Information Campaign on Infant Mortality in Denmark,” NBER WP 28621 / *Review of Economics and Statistics* (2022).

## 3. Kangaroo mother care (KMC)

### Immediate KMC for low-birthweight infants (multi-country RCT)

**Design:** Immediate, continuous skin-to-skin KMC from birth vs conventional care (incubator/warmer until stabilization, then KMC) in infants 1.0–1.799 kg. Five sites in Ghana, India, Malawi, Nigeria, Tanzania; 3,211 infants.

**Effect sizes (deaths in trial):**

- **28-day neonatal mortality:** 12.0% (191/1,609) in immediate KMC vs 15.7% (249/1,602) in control → **58 fewer deaths** in the intervention group (relative risk 0.75; 95% CI 0.64–0.89).
- **WHO scaling estimate:** If immediate KMC were adopted globally where currently only post-stabilization KMC is used, approximately **150,000 additional lives** could be saved per year.

**Source:** *NEJM* (2021), “Immediate Kangaroo Mother Care and Survival of Infants with Low Birth Weight” (NEJMoa2026486); WHO summary.

### KMC meta-analyses

**Effect:** Compared with conventional care, KMC is associated with about **32% lower neonatal mortality** (RR ~0.68; high-certainty evidence). Early initiation (within 24 hours) is associated with ~23% lower neonatal mortality vs late initiation. Greater benefit with ≥8 hours of KMC per day.

**Sources:** WHO ELENA; BMJ Global Health meta-analysis (e.g., 2023, e010728); WHO news (2021).

## 4. Community-based and home-based neonatal care (low- and middle-income countries)

### Home-based neonatal care by community health workers

**Effect:** Community health worker–delivered home-based neonatal care reduced **neonatal mortality by ~25%** (RR ~0.75) and perinatal mortality by ~22% in systematic reviews. In some subgroups (e.g., preterm or low-birthweight), larger relative reductions (e.g., 25–58%) are reported.

**Sources:** Nature/*Journal of Perinatology* systematic review; BMJ Global Health (e.g., preterm/low birth weight meta-analysis, PMC7896575).

### CHAMPION trial (India)

**Design:** Package of community health promotion and medical services for maternal and newborn care, cluster RCT in Telangana.

**Effect sizes (infant deaths saved):**

- **Neonatal mortality:** 24% lower in the intervention arm (5.2% vs 6.9%) → **92 fewer neonatal deaths** (95% CI 31–152) among ~5,798 intervention vs ~4,998 control children.

**Source:** PLOS Medicine, “CHAMPION cluster randomised trial…” (e1002324).

### Newhints (Ghana)

**Design:** Home visits by community volunteers promoting essential newborn care and care-seeking.

**Effect:** Large improvements in care-seeking for sick newborns (e.g., 77% vs 55% facility care) and in key practices (handwashing, bednets, transport planning). Impact on neonatal mortality was assessed in the trial; the intervention is cited as evidence for community-based neonatal care.

**Source:** Newhints cluster RCT (LSHTM Research Online).

### BCG and OPV at birth (Guinea-Bissau)

**Design:** Early BCG and oral polio vaccine at home within 72 hours of birth vs usual timing, cluster RCT.

**Effect:** **59% lower early infant mortality** (95% CI 8–82%); **21 fewer deaths** in the intervention arm (7 vs 28 deaths among 2,226 newborns).

**Source:** BMJ Global Health (e014044).

## 5. Facility-based and integrated care

### Safe Childbirth Checklist (Rajasthan, India)

**Design:** WHO-style safe childbirth checklist and implementation in facilities.

**Effect:** ~11% reduction in facility-based stillbirths and very early neonatal deaths. Scaling estimate: on the order of **~40,000 intrapartum-related deaths averted per year** if implemented across India.

**Source:** Rajasthan quasi-experimental study (MCNP Journal / BMC).

### Integrated Management of Neonatal and Childhood Illness (IMNCI) (India)

**Design:** Cluster RCT of IMNCI implementation.

**Effect:** Significant reductions in neonatal and infant mortality in the trial; exact “deaths saved” not restated here but supports integrated community/facility case management as an effective strategy.

**Source:** BMJ (e1634).

## 6. Other evidence-based interventions (meta-analyses, LMICs)

The following are associated with significant reductions in neonatal mortality in systematic reviews; effect sizes are usually reported as relative risks rather than absolute deaths saved:

- **Antenatal corticosteroids** for preterm birth: RR ~0.74–0.89.
- **Chlorhexidine skin cleansing:** RR ~0.72.
- **Single cord cleansing:** RR ~0.65.
- **Early BCG vaccination:** RR ~0.64.

Scaling these to “deaths saved” requires baseline mortality and coverage; the *Lancet* “End Preventable Deaths” style analyses (e.g., “Can Available Interventions End Preventable Deaths… at What Cost?”) provide population-level estimates for packages of interventions.

**Sources:** BMJ Global Health meta-analyses (e.g., PMC7896575); WHO guidelines; Obstetric Survey (2014) on ending preventable deaths.

## 7. U.S. equity-focused and quality-improvement initiatives

### Collaborative Improvement and Innovation Network (CoIIN) to Reduce Infant Mortality (U.S. South, 2011–2014)

**Design:** Multi-state quality improvement on safe sleep, smoking cessation, preconception health, preterm birth prevention, and risk-appropriate care.

**Effect:** CoIIN regions showed **larger improvements** in process and intermediate outcomes (e.g., early elective delivery −22% vs −14%; smoking cessation +7% vs +2%; back sleep +5% vs +2%; preterm birth −4% vs −2%). **No statistically significant difference** in infant mortality between CoIIN and non-CoIIN regions over the evaluation period—suggesting that short-run, system-level infant mortality impact may require longer follow-up or additional strategies.

**Source:** PMC5944875, “The Collaborative Improvement and Innovation Network (CoIIN) to Reduce Infant Mortality: An Outcome Evaluation From the US South, 2011 to 2014.”

### Best Babies Zone (BBZ), Fourth Trimester, Healthy Start

**Design:** Place-based (BBZ) and clinical (Fourth Trimester, Healthy Start) programs targeting racial inequities in infant mortality and maternal health.

**Effect:** BBZ and related initiatives are associated with reductions in racial inequities and improved birth outcomes (e.g., Healthy Start and birth weight); quantified “deaths saved” from published evaluations are limited. They are important for equity and are part of the portfolio of U.S. interventions.

**Sources:** UC Berkeley Public Health (BBZ); Healthy Start outcome studies (e.g., PMC4011095); state-level progress on Black–White gap (PMC5388953).

## Caveats

- **Attribution:** “Deaths saved” often comes from model-based projections (NFP), scaling assumptions (WHO KMC, Safe Childbirth Checklist), or trial-based absolute risk differences. Real-world impact depends on fidelity, coverage, and context.
- **Generalizability:** Many of the largest quantified effects are from low- and middle-income settings; NFP and safe sleep evidence is from the U.S. or high-income countries. Effect sizes are not directly transferable across settings.
- **Equity:** Several interventions (NFP, Denmark campaign, BBZ, Healthy Start) show larger benefits for disadvantaged or high-risk groups, which can reduce disparities if implemented with equity focus.

## 8. Wisconsin and U.S. disparity context (addendum)

### Wisconsin-specific evidence

**Tomlin et al. (2021)** used WISH data (2011–2016) to evaluate factors associated with racial inequity in infant mortality in Wisconsin. Crude and adjusted IMR were 13.7 and 9.1 for Black infants vs 4.8 and 5.3 for White infants. The disparity was due to (1) increased premature births among Black infants and (2) increased mortality among term Black infants. Among term Black infants, deaths due to **SIDS, accidents, and assaults** were markedly high—supporting a focus on modifiable causes (safe sleep, injury prevention) in addition to preterm birth prevention. The study cites Wisconsin’s Lifecourse Initiative for Healthy Families (LIHF) and Dane County efforts.

*Source: Tomlin BD, McAdams RM, Zapata JY, Kaluarachchi DC. High Black infant mortality in Wisconsin: factors associated with the ongoing racial inequity. J Perinatol. 2021;41:212–219. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41372-020-00906-6*

### National disparity and policy context

**KFF (2025)** summarizes U.S. racial disparities in maternal and infant health: Black infant mortality in 2023 was 10.9 vs 4.5 per 1,000 for White infants; disparities persist across income and education; structural racism and access to care are drivers; recent federal policy changes (Medicaid cuts, elimination of Safe to Sleep campaign, PRAMS pause) may widen disparities. This national context is relevant to Wisconsin’s high Black infant mortality and to state-level initiatives such as the Birth Equity Act.

*Source: KFF. Racial Disparities in Maternal and Infant Health: Current Status and Key Issues. Dec 2025. https://www.kff.org/racial-equity-and-health-policy/racial-disparities-in-maternal-and-infant-health-current-status-and-key-issues/*
