# Literature Review: Black Infant Mortality, Wisconsin Disparities, and Equity-Focused Interventions

**Date:** 2026-03-13  
**Query:** Black infant mortality disparity United States; Wisconsin infant mortality Black White Milwaukee; equity interventions; evaluation with small sample size

---

## Summary

Black–White infant mortality disparities in the United States have persisted for decades. Wisconsin has the highest Black infant mortality rate among U.S. states. The drivers are twofold: (1) higher rates of preterm birth and low birthweight among Black infants, and (2) higher mortality among term Black infants from modifiable causes (SIDS, accidents, assaults). Structural racism, access to care, and quality of care—not biology—underpin these gaps. Evidence on interventions that reduce infant mortality (NFP, safe sleep, KMC, community-based care) is strong in trials and some scaled programs, but effect sizes are modest in rate terms (e.g., 17–32% relative reductions). In Wisconsin, outside Milwaukee, the number of Black infant deaths is small; confidence intervals for rates are wide, so demonstrating a statistically significant intervention effect is difficult. Equity-focused U.S. initiatives (doulas, NICU quality improvement, CoIIN) show mixed or process-level effects; direct “deaths saved” estimates for Black infants in state or sub-state settings are limited. Focusing intervention and evaluation on Milwaukee—where most Black infant deaths in Wisconsin occur—aligns with the precision needed to learn what works.

## Key Papers

### Tomlin et al. (2021) — High Black infant mortality in Wisconsin: factors associated with the ongoing racial inequity

- **Main contribution:** First peer-reviewed analysis of Wisconsin WISH data (2011–2016) stratifying Black vs White infant mortality by gestational age and cause of death.
- **Method:** Births and deaths from WISH; direct adjustment for gestational age; comparison by cause of death, maternal age, education.
- **Key finding:** Crude IMR 13.7 (Black) vs 4.8 (White) per 1,000; adjusted IMR 9.1 vs 5.3. Disparity due to (1) more premature births among Black infants and (2) higher mortality among term Black infants. In term Black infants, SIDS, accidents, and assaults were markedly high.
- **Relevance:** Directly supports our project’s use of WISH and the argument that modifiable causes (SIDS, safe sleep, injury prevention) are targets. Cites Wisconsin life-course initiatives (LIHF) and Dane County efforts.

*Source: J Perinatol 41, 212–219 (2021). https://doi.org/10.1038/s41372-020-00906-6*

### KFF (2025) — Racial Disparities in Maternal and Infant Health: Current Status and Key Issues

- **Main contribution:** Overview of national racial disparities in maternal and infant health, drivers (structural racism, access, coverage), and recent policy threats (Medicaid cuts, Title X, Safe to Sleep cuts, PRAMS pause).
- **Method:** KFF analysis of CDC WONDER, NCHS, Pregnancy Mortality Surveillance System.
- **Key finding:** Black infants 2023 mortality 10.9 vs White 4.5 per 1,000; disparities persist across income/education; discrimination contributed to 30% of pregnancy-related deaths (CDC 2020); Medicaid covers a large share of Black and AIAN births.
- **Relevance:** National context for Wisconsin; supports framing disparities as structural and policy-sensitive.

*Source: KFF issue brief, Dec 3, 2025. https://www.kff.org/racial-equity-and-health-policy/racial-disparities-in-maternal-and-infant-health-current-status-and-key-issues/*

### Miller (2015) — Projected Outcomes of Nurse-Family Partnership Home Visitation During 1996–2013, United States

- **Main contribution:** National projection of NFP impact: ~500 infant deaths prevented by 2031 for 1996–2013 enrollees; cost ~$1.6B (2010), savings ~$3.0B.
- **Method:** Model-based projection from RCT evidence (Elmira, Memphis, Denver).
- **Key finding:** ~0.31 lives saved per $1M; Memphis RCT showed zero preventable child deaths in nurse-visited group vs 1.6% in control.
- **Relevance:** Only intervention in our review with a clear lives-saved-per-dollar estimate; directly relevant to U.S. equity discussion. Note: recent South Carolina RCT (JAMA) found no significant effect on birth outcomes in a Medicaid-eligible population.

*Source: Prev Sci 16(6):765–777. PMC4512284.*

### Altindag, Greve, Tekin (2022) — Public Health Policy at Scale: Government-Sponsored Information Campaign on Infant Mortality in Denmark

- **Main contribution:** Quasi-experimental estimate of nationwide safe-sleep campaign: 17.2% reduction in infant mortality; 11.6–13.5 deaths averted per 10,000 births; effects 11–14× larger for low-birthweight/preterm and lower-SES.
- **Method:** Danish register data; campaign rollout via universal nurse home visiting.
- **Key finding:** Translates to ~1.2–1.4 per 1,000 rate reduction; comparable to 17% relative reduction we use for CI-width comparison in Wisconsin.
- **Relevance:** Benchmark effect size for information/safe-sleep interventions; equity-relevant (larger effects in vulnerable groups).

*Source: NBER WP 28621; Rev Econ Stat.*

### NEJM (2021) — Immediate Kangaroo Mother Care and Survival of Infants with Low Birth Weight

- **Main contribution:** Multi-country RCT: immediate KMC reduced 28-day neonatal mortality (12.0% vs 15.7%); 58 fewer deaths in trial; RR 0.75 (95% CI 0.64–0.89).
- **Method:** Five sites (Ghana, India, Malawi, Nigeria, Tanzania); 3,211 infants 1.0–1.799 kg.
- **Key finding:** WHO scaling: ~150,000 lives/year if adopted globally where only post-stabilization KMC is used; meta-analyses suggest ~32% neonatal mortality reduction (RR ~0.68).
- **Relevance:** Upper end of effect sizes we compare to Wisconsin CI widths (~4.2 per 1,000 at 13/1,000 baseline for 32% reduction).

*Source: NEJM. NEJMoa2026486.*

### Byrd et al. (2007) — Infant mortality: explaining Black/White disparities in Wisconsin

- **Main contribution:** Earlier Wisconsin analysis (Matern Child Health J) decomposing Black/White infant mortality disparity.
- **Method:** Wisconsin vital records; regression and decomposition.
- **Key finding:** Part of the evidence base for Wisconsin-specific disparity; cited by Tomlin et al.
- **Relevance:** Historical Wisconsin context; may contain additional county-level or cause-specific detail.

*Source: Matern Child Health J 11:319–326. https://doi.org/10.1007/s10995-007-0183-6*

### Hirai et al. (CoIIN) — Collaborative Improvement and Innovation Network to Reduce Infant Mortality, US South 2011–2014

- **Main contribution:** Multi-state QI (safe sleep, smoking cessation, preconception, preterm prevention, risk-appropriate care); process and intermediate outcomes improved more in CoIIN regions, but no statistically significant difference in infant mortality.
- **Method:** Outcome evaluation; comparison CoIIN vs non-CoIIN regions.
- **Key finding:** Suggests system-level infant mortality impact may need longer follow-up or additional strategies; relevant to expectations for state-level programs.
- **Relevance:** Sets realistic expectations for short-run, population-level detectable effects in U.S. settings.

*Source: PMC5944875.*

### Doula RCTs (Enhanced Community Doula — Boston; Doula-Home-Visiting — Illinois)

- **Main contribution:** RCTs of doula services in safety-net settings (Boston 2023; Illinois, 312 women, 45% Black, 38% Latina). Illinois: improved childbirth prep, breastfeeding, safe sleep; no significant difference in cesarean, birthweight, prematurity.
- **Key finding:** Benefits on process and some outcomes; direct infant mortality impact not yet demonstrated in these trials; cohort study (17,831 deliveries) found 3–4 fewer preterm per 100 with doula care.
- **Relevance:** Equity-focused intervention with growing policy interest (e.g., Medicaid coverage of doulas); aligns with Birth Equity Act–type initiatives; more evidence needed on mortality.

*Sources: Health Equity (Liebert) 2023, PMC10507922; Matern Child Health J (Springer) doula-home-visiting.*

## Thematic Organization

### Theoretical / Conceptual Contributions

- **Structural racism and life course:** Disparities framed as rooted in racism and social determinants, not biology (KFF, Tomlin et al., Gee et al. life course; Collins & David birthweight by nativity). Weathering hypothesis and provider discrimination (CDC 30% of pregnancy-related deaths) support targeting systems and quality of care.
- **Reproductive justice:** KFF and advocacy literature tie maternal/infant equity to reproductive justice and broader social determinants; relevant to Wisconsin policy narrative.

### Empirical Findings

- **Wisconsin:** Black IMR ~3× White; driven by preterm and term mortality; term Black infants high on SIDS, accidents, assaults (Tomlin et al.). WISH remains the key data source; our project’s rates (WISH 1999–2023; summary tables use 2019–2023 average) extend this.
- **National:** Black infant mortality ~2–2.5× White (10.9 vs 4.5 per 1,000 in 2023); persistence across education/income; Medicaid covers majority of Black and AIAN births (KFF).
- **Interventions:** NFP (projected 500 deaths, ~0.31 per $1M); Denmark campaign (17.2%); KMC (32% RR); home-based neonatal care (~25%); doulas (process/breastfeeding/preterm in some studies); CoIIN (process gains, no significant mortality difference). Only NFP has clear $/life in U.S.

### Methodological Innovations / Constraints

- **Small samples and power:** Trials (e.g., BCG+OPV Guinea-Bissau) show wide CIs when enrollment or events are low; propensity score methods in survey data often leave many interventions without adequate confounder balance. For Wisconsin: outside Milwaukee, Black infant death counts (e.g., ~6/year Dane, ~19/year Rest of WI) imply wide CIs—consistent with our “CI width > effect size” argument.
- **Evaluation design:** Concentrating evaluation where event counts are largest (Milwaukee Black infants, ~55 deaths/year) improves feasibility of detecting modest effects (e.g., 17–32% relative reduction).

### Open Debates

- **NFP generalizability:** Memphis (primarily Black, strong effects) vs recent South Carolina RCT (no significant birth-outcome effect in Medicaid-eligible population)—replication and context matter.
- **Federal policy:** Cuts to Safe to Sleep, PRAMS, Medicaid, Title X (KFF 2025) may widen disparities; state-level initiatives (e.g., Birth Equity Act) operate in this context.

---

## Gaps and Opportunities

1. **Wisconsin-specific intervention evaluations:** No published RCT or rigorous quasi-experiment of an infant mortality intervention specifically in Wisconsin or Milwaukee with Black infant mortality as primary outcome. Opportunity: design evaluation for any new Milwaukee-focused program (e.g., doula expansion, safe sleep, NFP scaling) with pre-registered analysis and adequate power.
2. **Quantified “deaths saved” for equity-focused U.S. programs:** Most U.S. equity initiatives (BBZ, Healthy Start, doula Medicaid) report process or intermediate outcomes; few report infant deaths averted with confidence intervals. Opportunity: require outcome evaluation and standardized reporting for state/federal equity grants.
3. **Small-area inference:** Methods for combining or borrowing strength across geographies (e.g., Bayesian small-area estimation) could sharpen rate estimates for Dane and Rest of Wisconsin; we have not applied them yet.
4. **Cause-specific mortality in Wisconsin:** Tomlin et al. use 2011–2016; cause-specific breakdown for recent years (e.g. 2019–2023) (SIDS, prematurity, injury) would strengthen targeting (e.g., safe sleep vs preconception care).
